About The Project

Harnessing computer vision technology to secure food supplies and support sustainable farming practices.

Computer Vision in Agriculture

Manual identification of crop diseases requires expert oversight, which is often inaccessible to small-scale farmers in remote villages. This system simulates professional diagnostic expertise using structural image processing algorithms.

By executing Gaussian blurring to eliminate background noises, translating RGB coordinates into custom HSL/HSV channels, and isolating green tones, we expose hidden brown and yellow fungal lesions on the leaf surfaces. This visual contrast is evaluated using NumPy array masks to determine the infection severity metric.

"Early identification of leaf infections can prevent up to 40% of seasonal yield loss in crops like tomatoes, potatoes, and rice."

Disease Encyclopedia

Select a crop leaf condition to explore clinical symptoms, biological causes, and recommended prevention strategies.

Healthy Crop Leaf

Target Baseline: Normal Chlorophyll Foliage

A healthy leaf has consistent green coloring due to healthy chlorophyll distribution. There are no dark brown spots, water-soaked patches, or powdery residue.

  • Uniform leaf tissue texture and firmness.
  • Clean, natural vein structures without blackening.
  • No sign of leaf curling or pest infestation.

Maintaining crop health requires consistent watering, proper spacing, and balanced fertilizer application.

  • Ensure adequate daily sunlight depending on the crop.
  • Keep soil moist but well-drained to avoid root rot.
  • Prune weak branches to redirect nutrients to main stems.

Preventative steps are the best way to avoid crop diseases and ensure a healthy harvest.

  • Use disease-resistant hybrid seed varieties.
  • Rotate crops every season to prevent soil-borne pathogens.
  • Inspect fields weekly to catch early signs of pest activity.

Tomato Early Blight

Pathogen: Alternaria solani (Fungal)

This disease shows up as dark brown spots with concentric target-like rings on older, lower leaves first.

  • Concentric target spots on the leaf surface.
  • Yellow halos (chlorosis) forming around the brown lesions.
  • Stem spotting and dry rot near the fruit attachment area.

Early blight is caused by the fungus Alternaria solani. It survives the winter in soil, crop residue, and weeds.

  • Warm temperature ranges between 24°C and 29°C.
  • Frequent rainfall or high humidity over 85%.
  • Spores spread to other plants by wind and splashing water.

Manage early blight by keeping foliage dry and applying targeted organic treatments.

  • Apply copper-based organic fungicides early in the season.
  • Remove and burn infected leaves; do not compost them.
  • Use drip irrigation instead of overhead sprinklers.

Tomato Late Blight

Pathogen: Phytophthora infestans (Oomycete)

Late blight is a fast-spreading disease. It starts as large, dark green water-soaked spots that turn purplish-black.

  • Large water-soaked leaf spots turning dark brown.
  • Fuzzy white mold on the underside of leaves when humid.
  • Rapid collapse and browning of the entire plant canopy.

This disease is caused by Phytophthora infestans, an oomycete or water mold related to algae.

  • Cool, wet weather with temperatures between 15°C and 22°C.
  • Leaves remaining wet for over 10 consecutive hours.
  • Spores traveling long distances on wind currents.

Because late blight spreads rapidly, proactive management and monitoring are critical.

  • Spray organic neem oil or preventative bio-fungicides.
  • Immediately destroy and discard infected plant tissues.
  • Space plants widely to help the leaves dry quickly.

Rice Brown Spot

Pathogen: Bipolaris oryzae (Fungal)

This disease is characterized by numerous small, oval-shaped brown spots with light-colored centers on rice leaves.

  • Oval, uniform brown spots looking like sesame seeds.
  • Grey or white centers in older spots, surrounded by brown margins.
  • Grain discoloration and empty, light-weight hulls.

Rice brown spot is caused by the fungus Bipolaris oryzae. It is often linked to nutrient-deficient soils.

  • Nutrient-deficient soils, especially lacking potassium.
  • High relative humidity (over 90%) and leaf wetness.
  • Fungus surviving on infected crop residues or weed hosts.

Prevent rice brown spot by improving soil health and using clean, certified seeds.

  • Apply balanced soil fertilizers, focusing on potassium.
  • Treat seeds with hot water (54°C) or preventative bio-agents.
  • Maintain proper irrigation levels to prevent soil drying.
